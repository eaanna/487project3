Traumatic Brain Injury in Youth

Traumatic Brain Injury: Background

While TBIs have existed as long as man, treatment and research did not emerge until the 19th and 20th centuries. Citing new techniques for head injury treatment in the American Civil War, fatalities of TBIs have dramatically decreased from then onward. During World War I, the presence of blast related injuries allowed scientists and doctors to gain an insight on how various brain injuries affect certain parts of the brain. In World War II, rehabilitation techniques for TBI victims were explored for the first time for motor difficulties. Research continued in the 20th century, and TBI awareness accelerated in the 1970s. Shortly after, groundbreaking medical advances, such as the MRI and the CT Scan, allowed doctors to more accurately pinpoint damage to the human brain.

The 1990s and 2000s are considered the most important decades in the history of brain injury. Due to the Persian Gulf, Iraq, and Afghanistan Wars, the US and other militaries were prompted to conduct research on blast related brain injuries, and the combination of the injuries with PTSD and the effect on the cognitive and motor functions in the body. Additionally, the 2000s brought the first diagnoses of CTE, otherwise known as Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy, from athletes and soldiers who had obtained repeated head injuries years before and were suffering in older age due to the repeated trauma. The recentness of these discoveries makes TBI one of the most urgent topics in medicine, as the long term affects of brain injury and the commonality of injury is so expansive across all ages.

What is a TBI?

According to the CDC, "A TBI is caused by a bump, blow, or jolt to the head that disrupts the normal function of the brain. Not all blows or jolts to the head result in a TBI." TBI severity can vary from mild (otherwise known as a concussion or mTBI) to severe, and severity affects the overall damage to the brain.

The degrees of a TBI

TBI's are categorized into mild, moderate, and severe. After head trauma, it is recommended to visit a doctor to ensure no permanent or severe damage occured; especially if any symptoms, such as headaches, difficulty thinking, or loss of consciousness occur. Symptoms fall into one of four categories: thinking/remembering, physical, emotion/mood, and sleep. Symptoms are used to evaluate a patient to determine the severity of the TBI.

In Youth

As a whole, children and the elderly are the most at-risk populations to suffer from a TBI. For the elderly, falls are most common, while children suffer from both falls and being struck by objects unintentionally. Many child emergency visits due to a TBI are attributed to sports, and the vast majority of diagnoses end up as mTBIs, known as concussions.

As seen in the image, soccer uses a move called "heading" which invloves using the head to hit the ball mid-air. This move, as well as other moves in other contact and semi-contact sports, can increase the risk of a TBI due to impact. Regardless, football is still consistently the sport with the most emergency visits with a twenty-thousand plus lead against the second leading sport, basketball (data is from 2010-2016 in the US).

In the same time period (2010-2016), males consistently went to the ED at double the rate females did. For both males and females, ages 10-14 had the most visits, with surrounding data following a bell curve.

In 2014, falls were the most common reason for emergency department visits associated with TBI. Young children (ages 0-4) and the elderly (65-75+) were the most at-risk to suffer from fall related TBIs. Being unintentionally struck by an object was second (such as a baseball), and motor vehicle accidents were third. As of recent years, the number of emergency department visits related to TBI have grown as a whole.

Also in 2014, of the roughly 2.5 million emergency visits, roughly a third (812,000) were children ages 0-17. Only 3% (~23,000) ended up in hospitalizations and less than 1 percent of these visits result in death. It is important to note, however, that while many child TBIs are minor, it is known that repeated concussions are dangerous and can result in death or have long term effects, some of which are not completely understood.
